🧭 The 3 steps — from your therapist

1 · Notice. Signs it’s an intrusive thought: it’s very different from how you usually think, it’s disturbing or against your values, and it’s hard to control.

2 · Label. Step back and tell yourself: “I’m having an intrusive thought. It’s meaningless and it’ll eventually pass.” Then jot it below.

3 · Accept. Don’t fight it — fighting is gasoline on the fire. Let it run its course (that doesn’t mean you agree with it). Keep doing what you were doing; it fades on its own. Notice how repetitive it is — repetition makes it boring.

Remember: thinking something doesn’t make it real or important. A thought is not a fact, a reality, or a secret desire. Your thoughts don’t define you — we judge people by their actions, not their thoughts. And over 90% of people have disturbing intrusive thoughts; it’s a normal brain glitch.

🎯 Refocus in 3 steps

1. Name out loud what you were doing (“I’m in lecture”, “I’m cooking”).
2. Do the very next small step of that task — write one note, watch the next play, read the next recipe line.
3. If the thought knocks again: “I already wrote you down.” Back to step 2.

⏰ Worry window

If a worry keeps demanding attention, don’t debate it now. Tell it: “Noted — you get 10 minutes at 6pm.” Jot it here so it’s captured. Most worries feel much smaller when their appointment comes.

💡 Why this works (60-second read)

A thought is just a thought. It’s a mental event — not a fact, not an intention, not a prediction. Everyone gets strange intrusive thoughts; they only get sticky when we fight or analyze them.

Writing it once = externalizing. You take it out of your head, put it on the page, and return to the present. That’s the whole move. No story-building, no rabbit hole.

Rumination is not exposure. Mentally reviewing or arguing with a thought feeds it. Real exposure is doing a feared activity (like your night driving — the more you do it, the easier it gets). If exposure work for thoughts (ERP) interests you, plan it with your therapist — don’t DIY it.

Watch for rituals. If you notice urges to rewrite a thought until it “feels right”, reread entries for reassurance, or check this app repeatedly — that’s useful information, not failure. Mention it at your next session.

Body basics. Exercise is one of the best evidence-backed tools for anxiety, and 5–10 minutes of daily meditation compounds. Your therapist’s recommendations, worth keeping.

Missed the first moment? Noticing you went down the rabbit hole and coming back IS the skill. No pressure, no scorekeeping.
